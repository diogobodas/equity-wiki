Commercial Vehicle tariffs, finalized in November ‘25, supersede the 232 Metals tariffs.
The headwind to street estimates may be material: construction & ag > truck >
electricals. Not only will companies need to incorporate higher costs into the ’26 guide
issued in February, but it is also likely to spill over into ’27 given the lapping effect from
1Q26 and the 2-3 months of inventory cushion that pushes out the P&L hit to mid ’26.
Focusing on the per-unit impact of these changes on the bill of materials (BOM) of imported
finished goods only: 1) +6% for ag & construction equipment with 3% hitting in ’26 – they
are most hit because the ratio of metals as a % of value to metals as a % of weight is widest;
2) no change to truck tariffs, because Section 232 commercial vehicle tariffs supersede the
Section 232 metals tariffs; 3) Electricals are beneficiaries, seeing a 17% BOM decline, with
8% hitting in ’26, given their high metals content by value and preferred status, which yields
a 15% tariff instead of 25%.
The 4-factor framework for sorting who is most/least impacted. What matters most:
1) the share of revenues generated from foreign imports into the US; 2) whether the sector
in which the company resides is already subject to Section 232 sectoral tariffs (trucks are
insulated); 3) whether the company follows LIFO accounting vs. FIFO – LIFO leads to more
upfront tariff cost recognition; 4) the share of competitors who import – this creates a
pricing/margin umbrella for domestic producers/those less reliant on imports
Rank-ordering the companies. ETN and HUBB are best positioned – the changes are
likely to result in lower Section 232 costs, given the high metals content of the products
and the step-down from 50% to 15% tariff rate due to the special designation. PCAR’s
position is status quo, given Section 232 commercial vehicle tariffs supersede the metals
tariffs. As for the ag & construction OEMs, these are the most negatively impacted, though
we would force-rank OSK, AGCO, DE and CAT, followed by CMI (though this may change if
truck engines fall under the aegis of commercial vehicles tariffs). Perhaps the path of least
resistance is owning the service companies: URI, LGN, J, PWR.

UNIT ECONOMICS: QUANTIFYING THE TARIFF IMPACT
To quantify the impact of the new Section 232 rules on OEMs’ average bill of materials, we make the following assumptions:
1. The pre-tariff bill of materials totals $100
2. Steel content. For construction and ag OEMs, we assume 20% steel content in the average bill of materials. We triangulate
this from the following: a) the OEMs have indicated that raw steel is typically10% of COGS, and b) have indicated that it is
a higher % of their bill of materials, hence the arrival to 20%. Our confidence in this estimate is reinforced by numbers we
reach in the IEEPA + Old Section 232 rules, spanning August ‘25 to February ‘26. Our estimates get us to an $8 hit from
IEEPA and $10 hit from Section 232, or $18 total. This represents a 40-45% IEEPA, 55-60% Section 232 tariff split, which
was alluded by our coverage. We make a similar assumption for trucks/engines. For electrical equipment, we have very little
(to no) clarity. However, we assume steel content is less for your standard electrical switchgear than it is for a large excavator
or tractor - hence, the 10% steel content assumption. We also assume copper is the majority of content (~60%) with the
remaining being non-steel.
3. The good is entirely manufactured internationally and imported for sale in the US. We’re simply capturing the full impact
of updated tariffs, and for simplicity, this does not capture incremental factors that are relevant to determining company-
specific impacts such as % of US sales imported (which we lack clarity on across our coverage).

CLASS 8 TRUCKS & ENGINES
A key detail: the recent updates to Section 232 had no net effect for Class 8 trucks. The reigning Class 8 truck tariff was
established by presidential proclamation 10984 signed on October 17, 2025 and effective November 1, 2025. This invoked
Section 232 authority independently following its own investigation. The most recent updates effective April 6th, 2026 operate
exclusively. The Class 8 proclamation 10984 contains an explicit non-stacking rule meaning products subject to the medium-
and heavy-duty vehicles (MHDVs) tariffs cannot be subject to Section 232 tariffs on steel, aluminum, copper, lumber, etc., hence,
the recent updates had no effect on truck OEMs.
